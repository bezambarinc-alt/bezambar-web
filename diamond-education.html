Diamond Education

Master the fundamentals of diamond quality. Learn the 4Cs, shapes, anatomy, and everything you need to know to select your perfect diamond.

The Four Cs

Diamond quality is universally measured by four key characteristics: Cut, Color, Clarity, and Carat Weight. Together, the 4Cs form an objective standard for evaluating and comparing diamonds, allowing you to make an informed decision that suits your preferences and budget.

Each C plays a distinct role in a diamond's beauty and value. While all four are important, they affect a diamond differently. Understanding how they work together will help you find the perfect stone.

Cut

Cut is the most important of the 4Cs because it directly determines how light interacts with your diamond. A well-cut diamond will reflect light brilliantly and display exceptional sparkle, while a poorly cut diamond may appear dull despite excellent color and clarity.

What Cut Measures

Diamond cut is not about the shape (like round or cushion)—it refers to the quality of how the diamond is faceted. Cut evaluates:

  • Proportions: The relationship between the diamond's table, depth, crown angle, and pavilion angle
  • Symmetry: How precisely the facets are aligned and how uniform they are
  • Polish: The smoothness of the diamond's surface; microscopic scratches affect light return

Cut Grades

The Gemological Institute of America (GIA) grades cut on a five-point scale:

Understanding Cut Quality

  • Excellent: Maximum light reflection and brilliance. Optimal facet proportions and polish.
  • Very Good: Excellent light performance with minor variations in proportions.
  • Good: Good light reflection but with more noticeable proportional differences.
  • Fair: Moderate light return; more significant proportional compromises.
  • Poor: Minimal light reflection due to poor proportions and faceting.

For maximum brilliance and sparkle, we recommend selecting a diamond with an Excellent or Very Good cut grade.

Color

Diamond color refers to the absence of color in a diamond. The less color present, the more valuable the diamond. The GIA color scale grades diamonds from D (colorless) to Z (light color).

Color Categories

  • Colorless (D, E, F): Completely free of color. The rarest and most valuable.
  • Near Colorless (G, H, I, J): Color not visible to the naked eye but detectable under magnification. Excellent value.
  • Faint (K, L, M): Slight color visible to the trained eye.
  • Very Light (N, O, P, Q, R): Light color visible to the naked eye.
  • Light (S, T, U, V, W, X, Y, Z): Noticeable color. More affordable but with visible hue.

Fancy Colored Diamonds

Beyond the standard D-Z scale, diamonds can exhibit fancy colors such as intense yellows, pinks, blues, and fancy vivid colors. These rare diamonds are graded separately on a Fancy Color scale. Fancy colored diamonds often command premium prices, especially vivid pinks and blues, as they are exceptionally rare in nature.

For most buyers: We recommend selecting a diamond in the "Near Colorless" range (G-J) for exceptional value without visible color differences to the naked eye.

Clarity

Diamond clarity measures the absence of inclusions (internal imperfections) and blemishes (surface flaws). The clarity of a diamond directly affects its transparency and light performance. Most diamonds contain tiny imperfections; finding "eye-clean" diamonds—those with no visible flaws to the naked eye—at a reasonable price is possible at various clarity levels.

Types of Inclusions

Inclusions are natural characteristics formed during a diamond's creation deep within the earth. Common types include:

  • Crystals: Tiny mineral crystals trapped within the diamond.
  • Feathers: Internal fractures that resemble feathers under magnification.
  • Clouds: Groups of tiny particles creating a hazy area.
  • Needles: Thin, needle-like inclusions.
  • Pinpoints: Very small dots of mineral crystals.

Eye-Clean Diamonds

"Eye-clean" means the diamond shows no visible inclusions when viewed by the naked eye at normal viewing distance. Many VS1, SI1, and even some SI2 diamonds are eye-clean, allowing you to save significantly while maintaining visual perfection.

For best value: Consider VS2 or SI1 clarity grades, where many stones are eye-clean and offer excellent value without compromising appearance.

Carat

Carat weight is the measurement of a diamond's weight, not its size. One carat equals 200 milligrams (0.2 grams). Carat weight is the most straightforward of the 4Cs to understand, but its impact on price is more complex than the others.

How Carat Weight Works

Carat weight is measured with precision scales accurate to one one-hundredth of a carat (1/100). A diamond of 0.50 carats is referred to as a "half carat," while 0.75 carats is often called a "three-quarter carat."

Carat and Price: The Exponential Relationship

Important: Price does not increase linearly with carat weight. A 2-carat diamond costs significantly more than twice the price of a 1-carat diamond of similar quality. This is because larger diamonds are rarer; the larger the stone, the fewer high-quality examples exist in nature.

For example:

  • A 1.0 carat diamond might cost $5,000
  • A 1.5 carat diamond of similar quality might cost $12,000
  • A 2.0 carat diamond of similar quality might cost $20,000+

Carat Weight and Visual Size

A diamond's visual size depends not just on carat weight but also on its cut and shape. A well-cut diamond may appear larger than a poorly cut diamond of the same weight. Shallow or deep cuts can make a diamond appear smaller than its carat weight suggests.

  • 0.5 carat round diamond: approximately 5.2mm diameter
  • 1.0 carat round diamond: approximately 6.5mm diameter
  • 1.5 carat round diamond: approximately 7.4mm diameter
  • 2.0 carat round diamond: approximately 8.2mm diameter

Finding Your Sweet Spot

Rather than focusing solely on carat weight, consider how cut, color, and clarity also affect your diamond's beauty and value. A well-cut 1.2-carat VS1 diamond may be more beautiful and a better investment than a poorly cut 1.5-carat SI2 diamond of the same price.

Shape vs. Cut

Remember: "shape" and "cut" are different. Shape is the outline of the diamond, while cut refers to the quality of faceting and light performance. A diamond's shape doesn't determine its cut grade—a round diamond can have a poor cut, and an emerald cut can be excellent.

Diamond Anatomy

Understanding the structure of a diamond helps you appreciate how faceting affects its beauty and performance. Here are the key anatomical terms and measurements that jewelers and gemologists use:

Table
The largest facet on the diamond's top surface. Light enters primarily through the table.
Crown
The upper portion of the diamond above the girdle. Facets in the crown direct light into the diamond.
Girdle
The widest part of the diamond, separating the crown and pavilion. Sets the diamond's outline.
Pavilion
The lower portion below the girdle. These facets reflect light back out through the crown.
Culet
The small facet (or point) at the bottom tip of the diamond. A small culet is preferred to prevent chipping.

Key Measurements & Angles

  • Depth: The measurement from the table to the culet, expressed as a percentage of the diamond's width. Optimal depth is typically 58-62% for round brilliants.
  • Table %: The width of the table facet compared to the overall width of the diamond. Ideal range is 52-62% for round brilliant diamonds.
  • Crown Angle: The angle between the crown facets and the girdle. Typically 34-35° for optimal light performance in round brilliant diamonds.
  • Pavilion Angle: The angle between the pavilion facets and the girdle. Typically 40-41° for optimal light performance in round brilliant diamonds.
  • Polish: The quality of the diamond's surface. Evaluated as Excellent, Very Good, Good, Fair, or Poor.
  • Symmetry: How precisely the facets align and relate to each other. Also graded Excellent to Poor.
  • Fluorescence: Some diamonds glow under ultraviolet light. Can be None, Faint, Medium, Strong, or Very Strong. Strong fluorescence may affect the diamond's color appearance.

Facets

A round brilliant diamond typically has 58 facets (57 if the culet is a point): 1 table, 8 crown facets, 8 star facets, 16 girdle facets, 8 pavilion facets, and 8 lower girdle facets. Each facet is precisely angled to optimize light reflection and refraction, creating the diamond's sparkle and fire.
